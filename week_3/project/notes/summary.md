# Dementia

**Quick Overview**
| Aspect | Dementia | Alzheimer's disease (AD) |
|--------|----------|--------------------------|
| Definition | A clinical syndrome - a set of symptoms that affect memory, thinking, language, judgement, and everyday activities. Not a single disease. | The most common underlying neurodegenerative disease that leads to dementia (approx 60-80% of cases). Characterized by amyloid-beta plaques, tau tangles, neuronal loss. |
| Cause | Many possible conditions: AD, vascular disease, Lewy-body disease, frontotemporal degeneration, Parkinson's, TBI, infections, metabolic disorders, etc. | Primary disease process; biological changes begin years before symptoms appear. |
| Diagnosis | Clinical assessment of cognitive/functional decline; imaging & labs to identify cause. | Diagnosis when the clinical pattern of dementia matches typical AD progression and other causes are excluded; biomarkers (CSF amyloid/tau, PET amyloid) increasingly used. |
| Treatment | Symptom-targeted (e.g., cholinesterase inhibitors for AD, levodopa for Parkinson-related dementia, antihypertensives for vascular dementia). | AD-specific drugs (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine, memantine) and disease-modifying trials aimed at amyloid or tau. |
| Prognosis | Depends on underlying cause; some are reversible, others progressive. |
| Prognosis (AD) | Progressive, currently incurable; median survival after diagnosis ~8-10 years (varies). |

**How They Are Related**
- **Alzheimer's disease is a type of dementia.** All people with Alzheimer's meet the clinical criteria for dementia, but not all people with dementia have Alzheimer's.
- **Epidemiology:** Approximately 60-80% of all dementia cases are attributed to Alzheimer's disease.
- **Pathophysiology overlap:** The hallmark brain changes of AD (amyloid plaques, tau tangles) produce the cognitive-functional decline identified clinically as dementia.
- **Clinical practice:** Clinicians first identify the presence of dementia (cognitive/functional decline) and then work to determine the underlying cause; AD is the first condition evaluated.

**TL;DR**
- Dementia = syndrome (symptoms).
- Alzheimer's = the most common disease that produces those symptoms.
- All Alzheimer's patients have dementia, but dementia can arise from many other diseases.
